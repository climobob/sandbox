NOAA Coastal Ocean Forecast System

Real-Time Ocean Products

Ocean Model

The Coastal Ocean Forecast System (COFS) is based on an experimental, hydrodynamic ocean circulation model which has been developed jointly by the National Weather Service's Environmental Modeling Center, the National Ocean Service's Coast Survey Development Lab, Princeton University, and U.S. Navy. This three-dimensional model produces 24-hour simulations of temperature, salinity, surface elevation, and currents for a region off the U.S. East Coast from ~30 to 47N and out to 50W. The model is driven at the ocean surface boundary by heat, moisture, and momentum fluxes provided by NCEP 's Eta-32 mesoscale atmospheric forecast model. The ocean model is driven along its open (i.e. southern and eastern) boundaries by climatological estimates of temperature, salinity, and transport. The spatial resolution of the model varies from approximately 20km offshore to about 10km nearshore. The coastal boundary corresponds to the location of the 10m isobath. In the vertical, an 18-layer sigma (terrain-following) coordinate system is used with at least half the layers concentrated in the upper 100m. Tidal forcing is included in the model.

Data Assimilation Cycle

At 24-hour intervals, Gulf Stream north-wall-location data and satellite altimeter data are assimilated with an OI-based algorithm from Princeton University. Gulf Stream location data are provided by NAVOCEANO and sea surface height anomalies (SSHAs) derived from satellite altimeter data are provided by the NOAA Laboratory for Satellite Altimetry. These data are input for correction of the model's sea surface height field and assimilation into the subsurface temperature and salinity fields using correlation functions statistically derived from the model itself. Gulf Stream location data for the current day and TOPEX data from the prior 10-day orbital cycle, are used in this analysis step, which provides the input fields for the second analysis step.

Sea surface temperature (SST) data from in-situ and satellite observing platforms are assimilated into the updated fields from the first data assimilation step, in a nowcast/data assimilation cycle producing initial conditions for the 24-h forecast. In-situ observations are from fixed and drifting buoys, C-MAN stations, and ships. Remotely sensed observations are MCSST retrievals from the AVHRR sensor onboard the NOAA polar orbiting satellites. Surface atmospheric forcing is currently obtained from 3-hourly forecasts from the NCEP Eta-32 mesoscale atmospheric prediction model. In the near future, improved surface forcing will be obtained from 3-hourly analyses of the NCEP Eta Data Assimilation System (EDAS).
For more information about the data assimilation cycle contact Jean.Thiebaux@noaa.gov or John.Kelley@noaa.gov.

Forecast Cycle

The forecast cycle generates coastal ocean forecasts out to 24 hours. Surface forcing is obtained from the 3-hourly surface fields from NCEP 's Eta-32 mesoscale atmospheric prediction model.

Description of Output

The majority of COFS output fields are packed in the WMO standard GRIdded Binary (GRIB) format or in ASCII files. Brief descriptions of the different types of GRIB and ASCII files are given below. Recent COFS nowcasts and forecasts for variables at selected levels are given below. For the information on degribbing click here.

.igb file: This GRIB file contains nowcasts or the 24-h forecasts valid at 0000 UTC (8 P.M. EDT or 7 P.M. EST) on the native COFS grid.

.sgb file: This GRIB file contains 1-h nowcasts or forecasts of water levels and 1m currents on the native COFS grid for the 24-h length of the nowcast/data assimilation or forecast cycle.

.agb file: This GRIB file contains 24-h average and surface fields of daily extremes on the native COFS grid for both the nowcasts or forecasts.

.stdigb file: This GRIB file contains 24-h forecasts valid at 0000 UTC on a standard lat/lon grid. The grid characteristics are the following: dimensions are 332 x 210 with a horizontal resolution of 0.1 degrees with the lower left corner at 83.05 degrees W longitude and 26.35 degrees N latitude. Variables which have been interpolated to the standard grid are water level and temperature, salinity and currents at 31 Z-levels.

.el, .tnc, .tmd, .tms and .tnu files: These files contain hourly nowcasts or forecasts of water levels or SSTs at COFS grid points closest to observing stations. The hourly elevation values for all stations along the coast are contained in the .el files while the SST values are separated into four files by geographic areas (ie. tmd = Middle Atlantic area).

Plans

The surface forcing for the COFS nowcast/data assimilation cycle will be obtained from analyzed fields of the Eta Data Assimilation System (EDAS-32).

The daily output is undergoing evaluation by marine forecasters of NCEP's Marine Prediction Center in Camp Springs, MD. In addition, COFS products will be evaluated as part of the Coastal Marine Demonstration Project in June and July, 1999 and February and March, 2000.
